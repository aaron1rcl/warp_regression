# warp_regression

Likelihood-based time warping for regression and forecasting on cyclical econometric series.

---

## Warp regression

Many macroeconomic and ecological series exhibit **quasi-periodic cycles** whose timing drifts: business-cycle length varies, commodity booms arrive early or late, predator–prey peaks shift by years. These patterns are **cyclical but not seasonal**—there is no fixed calendar phase to difference out. Standard ARIMA or fixed-frequency harmonic models treat phase as exogenous; flexible nonparametric smoothers treat timing as unstructured noise. Neither gives a compact story: *a known-shaped driver, warped along the time axis*.

**Warp regression** posits that an observed series \(y_i\) is generated by **reading out a driver** \(x\) after a **monotone time warp**. At each index \(i\),

\[
\hat y_i = r\!\big(\mathrm{soft\_warp}(x,\, p)_i\big),
\]

where \(p(i)\) is a warp path (fractional indices into \(x\)), \(r(\cdot)\) is a readout (linear, MLP, trend+sine composition), and \(\mathrm{soft\_warp}\) linearly interpolates \(x\) at non-integer positions. The path \(p\) is low-dimensional—typically a B-spline–like knot parameterisation with \(K \ll n\) degrees of freedom—so the model separates **shape** (the driver) from **timing** (the warp).

### Why not just dynamic time warping?

**Dynamic time warping (DTW)** and its differentiable variants excel at *aligning* two sequences and at building predictive features after alignment. They are fundamentally **optimisation tools**: a warp is chosen to minimise a pairwise cost, not sampled from a generative model. That makes three econometric tasks awkward:

1. **Regularisation** — DTW costs are often hand-tuned; there is no native trade-off between fit and warp complexity analogous to a smoothness prior.
2. **Uncertainty** — a single optimal warp carries no forecast distribution; bootstrap over DTW is expensive and not tied to a generative law for future timing.
3. **Joint inference** — simultaneous learning of driver parameters, readout, and warp under one objective requires a **likelihood** (or penalised likelihood) that scores both fit and plausibility of the warp.

Warp regression supplies that analog: a **dual likelihood** with interpretable scales \(\sigma_y\) (observation noise) and \(\sigma_t\) (timing volatility), trained by gradient descent like any other torch model, with path samples for predictive bands.

## Model and inference

### Observation model (error likelihood)

Given warp path \(p\) and readout parameters \(\theta\),

\[
y_i \mid p, \theta, \sigma_y \sim \mathcal N\!\big(\hat y_i,\, \sigma_y^2\big),
\qquad
\hat y_i = r_\theta\!\big(\mathrm{soft\_warp}(x,\, p)_i\big).
\]

The **error term** (negative log-likelihood, reported as `obj_err`) is standard Gaussian NLL over residuals \(y_i - \hat y_i\). In log-price or log-count targets this plays the role of a homoskedastic innovation; \(\sigma_y\) is learned.

### Path model (terror likelihood)

Timing is encoded by **warp offsets** \(o_i = p(i) - i\) (identity path mode; other modes apply the same idea in stored coordinates). Offsets are forced smooth by a **piecewise Gaussian random walk** on a knot grid.

Place \(K\) knots at indices \(0 = \kappa_0 < \kappa_1 < \cdots < \kappa_K = n-1\) with segment width \(\Delta = n/K\). Between consecutive knots the increment

\[
\Delta o_j = o(\kappa_j) - o(\kappa_{j-1})
\]

is scored under a Gaussian RW with scale \(\sigma_t\). Rather than a discrete sum of squared increments (which would be a roughness penalty), the implementation uses the **expected log-likelihood** of a continuous-time Brownian bridge between knot values—matching the *terror* construction in WarpPureNumpy:

\[
\mathcal L_{\mathrm{terror}}(p) = \sum_{j=1}^{K} \mathbb E\big[\log p(\Delta o_j \mid o_{\kappa_{j-1}},\, \sigma_t,\, \Delta)\big],
\]

where the expectation integrates over the unknown within-segment path. Each segment contributes via a closed-form `expected_likelihood` (conditional posterior mean/variance and a Gaussian convolution `px_z`). The total **terror log-likelihood** (`terror_ll`) is differentiable in knot values, enabling joint backprop through \(p\) and the readout.

Intuition: \(\sigma_t\) sets how much cycle **timing** may bend between knots; large \(\sigma_t\) allows flexible warps, small \(\sigma_t\) pulls toward uniform spacing.

### Dual objective

Training minimises a convex combination controlled by **`fit_lambda`** \(= \lambda\):

\[
\mathcal J = \lambda \cdot \underbrace{(-\log p(y \mid \hat y, \sigma_y))}_{\texttt{obj\_err}}
\;-\;
(1-\lambda) \cdot \underbrace{\mathcal L_{\mathrm{terror}}(p)}_{\texttt{terror\_ll}}.
\]

\(\lambda \to 1\) prioritises fit (risk of overfitting the warp); \(\lambda \to 0\) trusts the path prior. In practice \(\lambda \in [0.5,\, 0.9]\) depending on series length and readout flexibility. Path knots \(B \in \mathbb R^K\) are mapped to offsets through a **zero-gradient construction** \(G(B)\) and linear interpolation, giving a smooth, low-dimensional warp without hard DTW constraints.

### Forecasting

For holdout and forward horizons, warp **offsets** are continued stochastically: from the train-end anchor, draw

\[
\Delta o_i \sim \mathcal N(0,\, \sigma_{\mathrm{step}}^2),
\qquad
\sigma_{\mathrm{step}} = \sigma_t \sqrt{\Delta},
\]

either per index (band construction) or per knot with piecewise-linear interpolation (smooth path draws). Each simulated path \(p^{(m)}\) yields a forecast curve \(\hat y^{(m)}\); percentiles form a **terror band**. Adding \(\mathcal N(0, \sigma_y^2)\) draws gives **error** and **combined** predictive bands (`build_forecast_bands`). The **point forecast** freezes the offset at the last training index—the deterministic analogue of the DTW alignment at the boundary.
